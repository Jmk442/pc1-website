  • Year 7 — plain language, concrete examples, minimal jargon.
  • University/Government — formal language, evidence-aware, policy-grade framing.

They are not "extra" chapters. They are paired readings.

How to use this book (3 reading paths)

Path A — Fast practical (recommended for most readers)

  1. Read the Year 7 section first.
  2. If you want more depth, read the University/Government section immediately after.
  3. Move to the next chapter.

Path B — Curriculum / teaching mode (schools, workshops)

  1. Use the Year 7 section as the lesson content.
  2. Use the University/Government section as the teacher notes: definitions, evidence framing, "why it matters" and "how to justify it".

Path C — Professional / policy mode (skip the basics)

  1. Skim the Year 7 section (30–60 seconds) to confirm the chapter's core point.
  2. Read only the University/Government section in full.
  3. Use it as citation-ready language for internal docs, strategy, or governance.

Quick navigation rule

  • If you feel the text is too simple, jump to University/Government.
  • If you feel the text is too dense, drop back to Year 7.

Why the book is built this way

This structure is deliberate: Clarity first (Year 7), Justification second (University/Government). It reduces re-reading and helps teams align, because everyone can share the same concepts at the level they need.

Part 1 — Why This Matters

The Problem · Chapters 1–3

Chapter 1 — What Happens When You Talk to AI

📗 Year 7 Version

You Press Enter. Then What?

You're sitting in class. You open ChatGPT on your laptop. You type:

"Tell me about space"

You press Enter. An answer appears in a few seconds. Easy.

But what actually happened in those few seconds? Where did your words go? What did the computer do with them? And why does it matter?

Let's follow your question on its journey.

Step 1: Your Words Become Numbers

AI doesn't read English. It doesn't read any language, actually. It reads numbers.

The moment you press Enter, your question gets chopped into tiny pieces called tokens. A token is usually a word or part of a word. The sentence "Tell me about space" becomes roughly four tokens. Each token gets turned into a number that the AI can work with.

Think of it like a translator at the United Nations. You speak English. The translator turns your words into something the other person can understand. Except here, the "other person" is a machine that only understands maths.

Step 2: Your Question Travels to a Data Centre

Your tokens don't stay on your laptop. They travel — through cables, across networks — to a data centre. A data centre is a building full of thousands of very powerful computers, all working together.

These buildings are enormous. Some are the size of several football fields. They run 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. And they need two things to keep running:

  1. Electricity — to power the computers
  2. Water — to cool them down, because they get incredibly hot

Your little question — "Tell me about space" — just activated a small part of one of those massive buildings.

Step 3: The Machine Starts Guessing

Here's the part most people don't know: AI doesn't look up answers. It's not like a search engine that finds a webpage. Instead, it generates an answer, one word at a time, by predicting what word should come next.

It's like the world's most advanced autocomplete. Your phone predicts the next word when you text. AI does the same thing — but with the knowledge of millions of books, websites, and documents built into its training.

The problem? Your question was vague.

"Tell me about space."

Space as in... outer space? Personal space? Storage space? Keyboard space bar? The AI doesn't know which one you mean. So it has to consider all the possibilities and make its best guess.

That guessing takes work. More possibilities = more calculations = more electricity = more water for cooling.

Step 4: The Answer Comes Back

The AI picks the most likely meaning, generates an answer token by token, and sends it back to your screen. The whole thing takes a few seconds.

But here's the catch: because your question was vague, there's a good chance the answer isn't what you wanted. Maybe it gave you a three-paragraph essay about the solar system when you just needed a definition of "outer space" for your science homework.

So what do you do? You try again. You retype. You rephrase. Each retry runs the whole process again — tokens, data centre, calculations, electricity, water.

Google has measured this. One AI text prompt uses about 0.24 watt-hours of electricity and 0.26 millilitres of water [1]. Tiny amounts. But multiply that by the 200 million people using AI every day, many of them retrying 3 or 4 times, and the numbers become massive.

The Key Insight

The quality of your question determines how hard the machine works.

A vague question makes the AI guess. Guessing costs energy. The answer often misses the mark, so you retry. Each retry costs more energy.

A clear, structured question does the opposite. It tells the AI exactly what you need. Less guessing. Better answer. First time. Less energy.

That's why learning to prompt well isn't just a tech skill — it's an environmental skill. And in the next chapter, we'll see just how big the retry problem really is.

Read the response. Was it what you needed?

Compare the two responses. Which one was more useful? Which one would you need to retry?

Discussion: If every student in Australia did Round 1 three times before getting a useful answer, how much extra electricity would that use? (Hint: there are about 4 million school students in Australia.)

1.1 — The Inference Pipeline: From Natural Language to Compute Cost

Every interaction with a large language model (LLM) follows a deterministic pipeline: tokenisation, embedding, forward pass through transformer layers, and autoregressive token generation. Understanding this pipeline is essential for grasping why prompt quality has a direct, measurable relationship with energy consumption.

When a user submits a prompt, the text is first tokenised — decomposed into sub-word units using a model-specific vocabulary. Each token maps to a numerical embedding vector. The number of tokens in both the input prompt and the generated output determines the computational load of the inference pass.

The transformer architecture processes these tokens through multiple attention layers, each requiring matrix multiplications proportional to the sequence length. The computational cost scales with:

  • Input token count — longer, more ambiguous prompts require more processing
  • Output token count — vague prompts generate longer, less focused responses
  • Attention window — the model must attend to all previous tokens when generating each new one

This is not abstract. Google's published environmental metrics quantify the cost of a median Gemini text prompt at 0.24 Wh of electricity, 0.03g CO₂e, and 0.26ml of water [1]. These figures represent a single inference pass. Retry cycles multiply them linearly.

1.2 — The Ambiguity Tax: How Vague Prompts Inflate Compute

Natural language is inherently ambiguous. The prompt "Tell me about space" activates probability distributions across multiple semantic domains. The model must resolve this ambiguity probabilistically.

This resolution process has measurable consequences:

  1. Token overhead: Unstructured prompts generate an estimated 3–4x more output tokens than structured equivalents for the same informational content.
  2. Relevance degradation: Without explicit constraints, the model's output is more likely to miss the user's intent — triggering retry cycles.
  3. Retry multiplication: Industry behavioural data suggests average users retry 3–4 times per task when using unstructured prompts.

Podder et al. (2026) demonstrated that structured prompting reduces inference energy consumption by 32–48% across GPT-4o, Cohere Command R+, and Mistral-7B [3].

1.3 — Scale: From Individual Prompt to Infrastructure-Level Impact

At the individual level, the energy cost of a single prompt is negligible. At population scale, it is not.

Consider the following extrapolation:

  • Daily active AI users (2025 estimate): 200+ million
  • Average retries per task (unstructured): 3–4
  • Energy per prompt: 0.24 Wh
  • Daily retry waste (conservative): 144–192 MWh

The critical insight is that prompt quality operates as a demand-side lever on this infrastructure trajectory. Unlike supply-side interventions (renewable energy, cooling efficiency, chip architecture), prompt optimisation requires no capital expenditure, no regulatory mandate, and no technological breakthrough. It requires education.

1.4 — Policy Implications

  1. AI Environmental Impact Assessments should include prompt quality metrics.
  2. Enterprise AI Governance frameworks should establish prompt quality standards analogous to code quality standards.
  3. Educational Policy should recognise prompt literacy as a foundational digital skill.
  4. ESG Reporting for AI-intensive organisations should include prompt efficiency metrics.

Chapter 2 — The Retry Problem

📗 Year 7 Version

The Loop Nobody Talks About

Here's something that happens millions of times a day, all around the world.

Someone opens ChatGPT. They type:

"Write me an essay about World War 2"

The AI writes something. It's... okay. But it's too long. It's about the wrong part of the war. It uses words they don't understand.

So they try again:

"Make it shorter"

Still not right. Now it's too short and it dropped the important bits.

"Add more about D-Day but keep it under 300 words"

Closer. But the tone is wrong — it sounds like a university textbook, not a Year 7 assignment.

"Make it simpler"

Four attempts. Four times the computer ran through millions of calculations. Four times the data centre used electricity and water.

This is the retry loop. And it's happening on a massive scale.

The Numbers

Remember from Chapter 1: one AI prompt uses about 0.24 watt-hours of electricity and 0.26 millilitres of water [1].

Now do the maths:

  • 200 million people use AI tools every day
  • Most of them retry 3–4 times per task
  • That's 600–800 million unnecessary prompts daily
  • At 0.24 Wh each = 144–192 megawatt-hours of wasted energy every day

To put that in perspective: 192 MWh is enough electricity to power about 640 Australian homes for a day. Wasted. Every single day. Just because people didn't know how to ask properly the first time.

The Email Lesson

When email was new, nobody taught people how to use it. People wrote entire letters as emails — "Dear Sir/Madam..." for a message that could have been one line. It was chaos. Then schools started teaching email skills.

AI prompting is at that same moment right now. Most people are doing the equivalent of writing a formal letter when they should be writing a text message.

What a Structured Prompt Looks Like

Let's go back to that World War 2 essay. Instead of four attempts:

"You are a Year 7 history teacher. Write a 250-word essay about the D-Day landings for a school assignment. Use simple language a 12-year-old would understand. Include one statistic and one quote. Use a formal but friendly tone."

That's one prompt. One attempt. One good answer. One quarter of the electricity. One quarter of the water.

Chapter 3 — The Hidden Cost

📗 Year 7 Version

The Invisible Machine

You can't see it. You can't hear it. But every time you use AI, a building the size of a shopping centre is working for you.

That building is called a data centre. And there are thousands of them, all around the world, running 24 hours a day.

How Big Is the Problem?

Electricity: In 2024, all the data centres in the world used about 415 terawatt-hours (TWh) of electricity [1]. That's about 1.5% of all the electricity on the entire planet.

Australia's total electricity consumption is about 250 TWh per year. So the world's data centres use more electricity than all of Australia. Every year.

And it's getting bigger. Goldman Sachs predicts data centre electricity demand will rise 165% by 2030 [2].

Water: A single large data centre can use up to 5 million gallons of water per day [3].

Carbon: Researchers at Cornell University estimate that by 2030, AI alone will produce 24–44 million metric tonnes of CO₂ per year [4].

The Part You Control

You can't change how data centres are built. But you can change how hard they have to work.

Researchers have measured this precisely. A landmark study published in 2026 found that structured prompting reduces the energy AI uses by 32–48% [5]. Not by building better hardware. Not by switching to solar panels. Just by asking better questions.

Chapter 4 — Context: Setting the Boundaries

📗 Year 7 Version

Imagine you get into a taxi.

You slam the door and say, "Take me there." The driver stares at you. Where? You haven't said which city you're in, which suburb you want, or even which direction to start driving. The driver could guess — but guessing wastes petrol, wastes time, and probably takes you somewhere wrong.

That is exactly what happens when you type a prompt into AI without context.

Context is the first letter in C.R.A.F.T. for a reason. It sets the boundaries of the entire conversation. It tells the AI:

  • What subject you are talking about
  • Who the audience is — are you writing for your teacher, your friend, or a scientist?
  • What constraints matter — how long, how detailed, what to include or leave out

Without context, the AI has millions of possible directions it could go. With context, you narrow it down to the handful that actually matter to you.

Watch what happens with a real example:

Without ContextWith Context
"Write about climate""Write a 200-word summary of how data centres affect climate, for a Year 7 science class in Australia"
Result: 800 words covering ice ages, greenhouse gases, polar bears, deforestation, and ocean currents. Half of it irrelevant. You delete it and try again.Result: 200 words focused on data centres, electricity use, and heat — exactly what you needed. First attempt. No retry.

That first attempt? It used electricity. A computer somewhere processed your words, generated a response, and sent it back. When you deleted it and tried again, that electricity was wasted. The computer did work for nothing.

Now multiply that by millions of people, every day, all typing prompts without context.

The three parts of good context:

  1. Subject — What topic are you asking about? Be specific. "Climate" is vague. "How data centres affect climate" is precise.
  2. Audience — Who is going to read this? A 12-year-old needs different language than a professor.
  3. Constraints — How long should it be? What should it include? What should it leave out?

Think of it this way: the more you tell the AI about your world, the less it has to guess. And every guess it doesn't have to make is energy it doesn't have to spend.

4.1 — Context as Search Space Reduction

Large language models generate output by sampling from probability distributions across their entire parameter space. An uncontextualised prompt — "Write about climate" — activates competing semantic clusters spanning atmospheric science, policy, economics, biology, and geography. The model must resolve this ambiguity probabilistically, producing outputs that are broad, hedged, and frequently misaligned with user intent.

Context operates as a pre-generation constraint mechanism. By specifying domain, audience, and parameters before the model begins token generation, the user effectively prunes the inference search space. The model allocates attention weights to a narrower semantic region, producing outputs that are more relevant, more concise, and less likely to require regeneration.

The relationship between context specificity and output efficiency is measurable across three dimensions:

Context alone does not achieve this full reduction, but it is the prerequisite upon which the remaining C.R.A.F.T. elements build.

4.3 — The Three Dimensions of Effective Context

Domain specification defines the subject boundary. "Climate" activates the entire semantic field. "The energy consumption of hyperscale data centres and their contribution to global carbon emissions" activates a precise subset. The narrower the domain, the fewer competing token distributions the model must resolve.

Audience specification calibrates register, vocabulary complexity, and assumed knowledge. A prompt specifying "for a policymaker with no technical background" produces fundamentally different output than one specifying "for a peer-reviewed journal submission." Without audience specification, models default to a mid-register generalist voice that satisfies neither audience.

Parameter specification defines output constraints — length, scope, inclusions, exclusions. These operate as hard boundaries on the generation process. A 200-word limit prevents the token-expensive tendency of LLMs to over-generate when unconstrained.

4.4 — Enterprise Context Standards

Organisations deploying AI at scale face a compounding problem: hundreds or thousands of users, each providing inconsistent context, each generating variable-quality outputs, each retrying at different rates. The aggregate compute waste is substantial.

Context standardisation addresses this through:

  • Domain glossaries — Agreed terminology that eliminates ambiguity. When every user in a legal team uses "Australian Consumer Law, QLD jurisdiction" rather than "consumer rights," output consistency improves measurably.
  • Audience templates — Pre-defined audience profiles ("board-level executive summary," "technical implementation team," "external regulator") that can be selected rather than written from scratch.
  • Constraint libraries — Reusable parameter sets for common tasks. A "quarterly report" constraint set might specify: 1,500 words, executive summary + 4 sections, current quarter data only, no forward projections without disclaimer.

These are not theoretical recommendations. They are implementable at the organisational policy level and directly reduce the per-prompt energy cost of AI deployment.

4.5 — Context and the Progressive Refinement Pipeline

Context is positioned first in C.R.A.F.T. because every subsequent element depends on it. Role assignment without context produces a generic expert. Action specification without context produces a correctly-named but incorrectly-scoped task. Format and tone, applied to a contextless prompt, produce well-shaped and well-voiced outputs that miss the point entirely.

The cascade is directional: context constrains everything downstream. This is why the energy savings compound — each subsequent element operates on an already-narrowed search space, rather than attempting to compensate for the ambiguity that context should have resolved.

Chapter 5 — Role: Assigning the Expert

📗 Year 7 Version

Imagine you could ask any expert in the world a single question.

A brain surgeon. A NASA engineer. A pastry chef. A criminal lawyer. A marine biologist. You walk up, ask your question, and get an answer shaped by decades of training and experience.

That is what the R in C.R.A.F.T. gives you.

When you tell an AI to act as a specific type of expert, something changes in the way it answers. It doesn't just use different words — it thinks differently. A doctor answering a health question will organise information around diagnosis, symptoms, and treatment. A scientist answering the same question will organise around evidence, methodology, and data. A journalist will organise around who, what, when, where, and why.

Same question. Three completely different answers. All useful — but only one is the answer you actually needed.

Without a role, AI defaults to "helpful assistant." That sounds fine, but it means the AI tries to be a little bit of everything and ends up being deeply nothing. The output is generic. It covers the basics. It doesn't go deep enough. So you read it, frown, and type: "No, I meant explain it like you're a scientist..."

That second prompt? That's a retry. That's wasted energy. That's a computer doing the work twice because you didn't set the role the first time.

The role builds on context. In Chapter 4, you told the AI what world it's operating in. Now you're telling it who it should be in that world.

Context OnlyContext + Role
"Write about photosynthesis for a Year 7 class""You are a Year 7 biology teacher who uses everyday analogies. Write about photosynthesis for your class."
Result: Accurate but textbook-style. Reads like a Wikipedia summary. Students lose interest by paragraph two.Result: "Imagine a leaf is a tiny kitchen. Sunlight is the oven, water is one ingredient, CO₂ is another, and the recipe makes glucose — the plant's food." Students get it immediately.

The first answer is correct. The second answer is useful. The difference is the role.

Choosing the right role:

  • Match the role to your need. If you want a simple explanation, pick a teacher. If you want a detailed analysis, pick a researcher. If you want advice, pick a professional who gives that kind of advice.
  • Be specific. "Act as a doctor" is okay. "Act as a GP with 15 years of experience in chronic disease management" is much better.
  • Stack it on context. The role only works well if the context is already set. A teacher without a subject is just a person standing in an empty classroom.

Why does role-setting save energy?

Because without it, you get a generic answer, then you retry with a role. Two prompts instead of one. Twice the electricity. Twice the water to cool the server. If you set the role from the start, the AI gets it right the first time — and the planet saves a tiny bit of energy, multiplied by millions of users.

5.1 — The Mechanics of Role-Priming

Role assignment in large language models is not metaphorical. When a prompt includes an instruction such as "Act as a senior credit risk analyst," the model's attention mechanism shifts weight toward token sequences associated with that domain's discourse patterns, terminology, reasoning structures, and output conventions.

This is observable in output: a prompt with role-priming produces responses that exhibit domain-specific vocabulary, field-appropriate hedging patterns, and structural conventions (e.g., a clinician produces SOAP-formatted notes; a legal professional produces numbered-paragraph submissions). The model is not "pretending" — it is activating a subset of its training data that corresponds to the specified expertise.

5.2 — Role-Priming and Inference Efficiency

Role specification reduces inference cost through two mechanisms:

  1. Vocabulary narrowing. A role-primed model draws from a constrained vocabulary distribution. A "senior financial analyst" prompt suppresses casual language tokens and elevates technical financial terminology, reducing the entropy of each token selection and marginally accelerating generation.
  2. Structural priming. Experts produce structured output by convention. Clinicians write in structured notes. Lawyers write in numbered paragraphs. Analysts write in executive summaries with supporting detail. Role-priming triggers these structural patterns, reducing the need for separate format specification (though format remains valuable as an explicit reinforcement — see Chapter 7).

The combined effect: role-primed prompts produce outputs that are more likely to be accepted on first generation, reducing the retry rate that drives compute waste.

5.3 — Role Libraries for Institutional Deployment

Organisations can systematise role-priming through role libraries — curated sets of pre-defined expert personas for common tasks.

A healthcare organisation's role library might include:

  • Clinical roles: GP, specialist, nurse practitioner, pharmacist, allied health professional — each with typical output patterns and register expectations

One word changes everything.

6.1 — Action Verbs and Token Efficiency

The action element of C.R.A.F.T. specifies the precise cognitive operation the model should perform. This is not a stylistic preference — it is a direct determinant of output length, structure, and generation cost.

Vague action language ("tell me about," "help me with," "write something on") forces the model to infer intent. The inference process is probabilistic: the model hedges by producing multiple types of output — explanation, analysis, listing, and commentary — in a single response. The result is over-generation: more tokens than needed, lower relevance, higher retry probability.

Precise action language ("compare," "evaluate," "classify") constrains the generation to a single cognitive operation. The model produces a focused output aligned with the specified task type.

6.3 — The One-Verb Rule

Multi-action prompts ("explain and compare and also suggest") create competing generation objectives. The model must allocate attention across multiple task types simultaneously, producing outputs that partially satisfy each but fully satisfy none.

The one-verb rule is a design constraint, not a limitation: one prompt, one primary action verb. If a task requires multiple cognitive operations, decompose it into sequential prompts, each with a single action. The total token count across sequential single-action prompts is consistently lower than a single multi-action prompt — because the multi-action prompt produces redundant text as the model transitions between task types.

This principle extends to enterprise workflow design: complex analytical tasks should be decomposed into prompt chains, each step performing one action on the output of the previous step. This is more token-efficient, more auditable, and produces higher-quality intermediate outputs.

6.4 — Action in the C.R.A.F.T. Pipeline

  • "You are an energy policy advisor to the Australian Government. Analyse the energy consumption of Australian data centres and their growth trajectory to 2030" (context + role + action) → Focused, policy-relevant, actionable

Each preceding element shapes how the action is interpreted and executed. The action verb is the trigger; context and role are the aim.

7.1 — Format as Pre-Generation Structuring

Format specification operates at a critical point in the generation pipeline: it determines the structural template before token generation begins. This is fundamentally different from post-hoc reformatting, which requires full regeneration.

When a user specifies "present as a table with four columns," the model's generation process is constrained to a tabular structure from the first token. The attention mechanism allocates resources to producing cell-appropriate content — concise, comparative, structured. The output is shorter, more information-dense, and more likely to be accepted on first generation.

Without format specification, the model defaults to its most probable output structure: continuous prose. Prose is the highest-entropy format — it permits the widest range of token sequences, generates the most tokens per unit of information, and is the least likely to match a user's specific structural needs.

Because AI doesn't automatically know who's going to read its output. 8.1 — Register as the Final Constraint

Tone — or more precisely, register — is the final element in C.R.A.F.T. because it operates on output that is already contextualised, role-informed, task-directed, and structurally defined. Tone does not change the informational content of the output. It calibrates the linguistic register to match the intended audience.

Register calibration involves three linguistic dimensions:

  1. Vocabulary complexity — The lexical density and domain-specificity of word choice. A patient-facing tone uses common vocabulary ("heart attack"); a clinical tone uses technical terminology ("acute myocardial infarction").
  2. Sentence structure — The syntactic complexity of output. Formal registers employ longer sentences with subordinate clauses and passive constructions. Accessible registers use shorter, active sentences.
  3. Hedging and authority — The degree of certainty and directness. Academic registers hedge ("the evidence suggests"); executive registers assert ("the data confirms"); educational registers simplify ("this means").

8.2 — The Cost of Tone Mismatch

Tone mismatch is the most subjective retry trigger in LLM interaction, but it is also one of the most frequent. Users who receive technically correct output in the wrong register face two options:

  1. Manual rewriting — The user edits the output to adjust tone. This preserves the compute investment but requires human labour proportional to output length.
  2. Full re-prompting — The user requests the same content in a different tone. This discards the original output entirely, requiring full regeneration.

Neither option is efficient. Both are avoidable through tone specification in the initial prompt.

Too casual for purposeRole-priming set a casual personaRe-prompt with explicit formality instruction

8.3 — Institutional Tone Standards

Organisations produce AI-generated content for multiple audiences simultaneously: internal teams, executives, regulators, customers, and the public. Without tone standardisation, each user's prompt produces output in a different register, creating inconsistent organisational voice.

Tone guides address this by defining register specifications for each audience level:

  • Board/Executive level — Concise, assertive, data-led. No hedging. Recommendations stated directly. Maximum 2 pages.
  • Regulatory/Compliance level — Formal, precise, citation-heavy. Passive voice acceptable. Full evidence trail. Structured per regulatory template.
  • Internal team level — Clear, direct, action-oriented. Active voice. Bullet points over prose. Task-focused.
  • Customer-facing level — Warm, professional, jargon-free. Empathetic framing. Solution-oriented.
  • Public communication level — Accessible, engaging, inclusive. Short sentences. Concrete examples. No assumed knowledge.

These register specifications function as reusable prompt components, analogous to the context templates (Chapter 4), role libraries (Chapter 5), and format libraries (Chapter 7) discussed earlier. Together, they form a complete organisational prompt infrastructure.

Tone is positioned last in C.R.A.F.T. because it is the most dependent element. Without context, tone has no audience to calibrate to. Without role, tone conflicts with the persona's natural register. Without action, tone has no task to voice. Without format, tone floats without structural anchoring.

With all four preceding elements set, tone operates as a final-pass filter on an already-constrained generation. The model has been directed to the right domain, given the right expertise, assigned the right task, shaped into the right structure — and now tone ensures it speaks in the right voice.

Chapter 21 — The Email Parallel: Why This Must Happen Now

Appendix D — Glossary

Audience — Who you're writing for. The intended reader profile that determines register, assumptions, and acceptable uncertainty.

C.R.A.F.T. — A five-part checklist to write clearer prompts: Context, Role, Action, Format, Tone. A progressive constraint method for specifying tasks pre-generation.

Inference — The "thinking step" where the AI turns your prompt into an answer. Model execution (forward pass + token generation) that consumes compute, energy, and cooling resources per run.

Inference run — One time the AI has to "do the work" to produce an output. A single model call within a workflow.

Retry loop — When you keep asking again because the first answer wasn't what you needed. Re-prompt cycles that multiply inference cost without increasing value proportionally.

Token — A chunk of text the AI reads (a word or part of a word). The basic unit of model input/output; inference cost scales with total tokens processed and generated.
