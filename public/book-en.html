Dual Reading Levels

This book is written at two levels simultaneously. The Year 7 level uses plain English, short sentences, and clear examples — suitable for students, parents, and anyone new to AI. The Professional / Managerial level is structured, precise, and industry-aware — suitable for managers, professionals, and technical users who want the full depth of the PC1 method.

You do not need to read both levels. Choose the one that feels right for you. Both arrive at the same conclusion.

The Hidden Cost

The Fear Is Not New

Every significant tool in human history has been met with the same response: fear.

When one person's neighbour first used a wheel, there was someone watching who felt something shift. When horses replaced walking, when the printing press arrived, when the motor car appeared on roads built for horses, when colour television came into the living room — in every case, there were people who felt that something was being taken from them. That control was slipping. That the world they understood was being replaced by one they did not.

They were not wrong to feel that. The shift was real. But in every case, the people who learned to use the new tool did not lose. The scribes who learned to print prospered. The horse-breeders who moved into motor vehicles built new industries. The television viewers who adapted found more of the world open to them than they had before.

AI is the current version of this story. And the fear people feel — about losing their creative voice, their job, their relevance, their control over their own thinking — is legitimate. It deserves to be taken seriously, not dismissed.

This book is not an argument that AI is good or inevitable or harmless. It is an argument that the people who learn to use it clearly, ethically, and precisely will be more capable than those who do not — in the same way that literacy made people more capable than those who could not read, and email literacy made people more effective than those who could not write a clear message.

The Three Hidden Costs

Cost 1
Time and Productivity

The hours lost to retrying, rewording, and reformatting bad AI outputs. Multiplied across a team or organisation, this becomes a serious drag on performance.

Cost 2
Energy and Environment

Every AI query has an energy and water cost. Poor prompting means more queries, more retries, more compute — a hidden environmental bill that compounds at scale. This is covered in detail in Part 4.

Cost 3
Creative Rights

The AI systems you use today were trained on vast quantities of human creative work — writing, art, music, code, translation, journalism — much of it absorbed without the knowledge or consent of the people who created it, and without any payment or attribution going back to them.

When you ask an AI to write in the style of a named author, produce art like a named artist, or compose music similar to a named musician — you are drawing on that reservoir. The original creator receives nothing.

Most people using AI for creative work are participating in this system without realising it. This is the third hidden cost — and it is the one that troubles thoughtful people most deeply.

The PC1 response to this is the E.T.H.I.C.S. lens — introduced fully in Chapter 9. It does not solve the systemic problem. But it gives you a principled, conscious position within it.

What Pure Data Means

There is one thing the prompts you build with PC1 produce that does not depreciate.

When you use C.R.A.F.T. to structure a request, the output reflects your thinking — your context, your role definition, your specific task, your required format, your calibrated tone. The better you ask, the more the output is shaped by you. That is not losing control to AI. That is the opposite of it. It is your thinking, made more precise, made more executable, made more durable.

A well-built prompt does not go stale. The methodology — how to ask clearly — remains valid regardless of which AI tool processes it, regardless of what version it runs on, regardless of what comes next. That is what makes PC1 a lasting investment rather than a tool that dates with the software.

Pure data is strong data. It does not need decoration. It does not need to be clever. It needs to be clear, structured, and honest. That is what C.R.A.F.T. builds. That is what this book teaches.

C — Context

C
What is Context?

Context is the background the AI needs to understand your situation. It tells the AI what the project is, who you are, what constraints apply, and what information it should draw on.

Without context, the AI makes assumptions — and its assumptions are almost always wrong for your specific situation.

Include: project name, audience, domain, relevant data, any documents or previous answers to reference, and any constraints (word count, legal rules, reading level).

Example: "I am writing a report for a Year 10 science class about climate change. The audience is 15-year-old students in Australia. Keep explanations simple."

Role: Assigning the Expert

[ PLACEHOLDER — Replace with final Chapter 5 prose. ]

R — Role

R
What is Role?

Role tells the AI what persona or expertise to adopt. When you assign a role, the AI adjusts its vocabulary, depth, assumptions, and style to match the persona you described.

Examples:

  • "You are a secondary school science teacher with 15 years' experience."
  • "You are a senior software engineer specialising in Python security."
  • "You are a plain-English legal writer for non-lawyers."

The more specific the role, the more targeted the response.

Action: Naming the Task

[ PLACEHOLDER — Replace with final Chapter 6 prose. ]

A — Action

A
What is Action?

Action is the explicit instruction — what you want the AI to do, step by step. AI tools respond best to numbered, sequenced instructions rather than vague requests.

Structure your Action as steps:

  1. Step 1: Clarify — ask if you need any information confirmed before proceeding.
  2. Step 2: Analyse — describe your reasoning or approach.
  3. Step 3: Execute — deliver the final output.

If the task has multiple stages, number each one. AI tools follow numbered lists reliably.

Chapter 7

Format: Shaping the Output

[ PLACEHOLDER — Replace with final Chapter 7 prose. ]

F — Format

F
What is Format?

Format tells the AI how to structure and present its answer. Without format instructions, AI tools default to a style that may not match your needs.

Format options include:

  • Headings and subheadings
  • Bullet point lists
  • Numbered lists
  • Tables
  • Code blocks (for technical output)
  • Length limits ("under 300 words", "one paragraph only")
  • Output type ("in JSON", "as a PDF-ready summary", "as a slide-by-slide script")

Tone: Calibrating the Voice

[ PLACEHOLDER — Replace with final Chapter 8 prose. ]

T — Tone

T
What is Tone?

Tone is the writing style and voice. It covers formality, warmth, technicality, and reading level. When you specify tone, the AI calibrates its vocabulary and sentence structure to match.

Tone examples:

  • "Formal and professional — no contractions."
  • "Friendly and encouraging — suitable for a 12-year-old."
  • "Precise and technical — assume the reader has an engineering background."
  • "Plain English — avoid jargon. Explain terms if you must use them."
  • "Concise — one idea per sentence. No filler."

Integration: The Complete System

[ PLACEHOLDER — Replace with final Chapter 9 prose. ]

C.R.A.F.T. — The Complete Framework

C
Context

Briefly state all relevant background. Include any data, documents, or links the AI should use.

R
Role

Define the AI's persona, expertise, and attitude. The more specific, the better.

A
Action

Outline exactly what you want the AI to do. Number each step.

F
Format

Specify output structure — headings, bullets, length, code blocks, tables.

T
Tone

State the writing style — formal or casual, technical or plain English, concise or detailed.

The D.E.T. Extension — For Software and Technical Tasks

When your task involves code, software, automation, or any technical build, append a D.E.T. checklist to your C.R.A.F.T. prompt. D.E.T. stands for Dependencies, Environment, and Testing.

D.E.T. — For Technical and Build Tasks

D
Dependencies

List required libraries, packages, or tools and their versions. Example: numpy>=1.20, express.js 4.x

E
Environment

Note any API keys, environment variables, or configuration requirements. Example: Set OPENAI_API_KEY in .env, database URL required.

T
Testing

Specify test framework and criteria. Example: Include unit tests using pytest achieving at least 80% coverage.

The E.T.H.I.C.S. Lens — For Creative Work and Authorship

When your task involves creative writing, translation, voice work, publishing, music, or any context where human authorship and creative rights matter, append an E.T.H.I.C.S. block to your C.R.A.F.T. prompt. It runs the same way D.E.T. does for technical builds — it does not replace C.R.A.F.T., it deepens it for the specific demands of creative work.

E.T.H.I.C.S. stands for Explicit Authorship, Transparency, Human leads, Integrity, Credit, and Sovereignty.

E.T.H.I.C.S. — For Creative Work, Authorship, and Rights

E
Explicit Authorship

State from the start who is the author of this work. The human. Establish this as a hard constraint before any creative work begins. The AI has no authorship claim on anything produced in this session.

Example instruction: "All work produced here is authored by [name]. You have no authorship claim on any output, draft, or idea that emerges in this session."

T
Transparency

The AI must acknowledge it cannot fully verify the origins of its training data. Where creative content is requested, it must flag this honestly. It may not present training-derived material as original or independently verified.

Example instruction: "If you cannot verify the specific origin of a fact, phrase, or creative approach — say so before continuing."

H
Human leads

Human creative vision, voice, and original expression are the primary material. AI produces structure, research, and scaffolding only. It does not write the final creative work — it prepares the ground for the human to write it.

Example instruction: "When I ask you to write something, give me a bare scaffold for me to build from — not finished prose."

I
Integrity

Do not mimic the specific style of named artists, writers, musicians, or creators. If asked, refuse and explain why. Protect the integrity of both the named creator and the human you are assisting.

Example instruction: "Do not generate content in the style of any named author, artist, or musician. If I ask you to, flag it and suggest an alternative."

C
Credit

When content draws on recognisable traditions, genres, or works, acknowledge it. Do not present AI-derived content as wholly original. Where specific works are relevant, name them. Honour the shoulders the work stands on.

Example instruction: "When relevant sources or known works inform what you produce, name them. Do not obscure what you drew on."

S
Sovereignty

The human retains full creative and legal ownership of everything in this session. The AI does not share authorship. The output belongs entirely to the human.

Example instruction: "I own this session's output fully. You will not be credited. That is correct and proper."

Three lenses — one system

C.R.A.F.T. is the base for all prompts.
Add D.E.T. when the task is a technical build, software, code, or automation.
Add E.T.H.I.C.S. when the task involves creative work, authorship, translation, voice, or publishing.
Both can be added to the same prompt when the work crosses both territories.
