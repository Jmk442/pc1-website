A Note from John

Before you start

Deborah — thank you for taking this on. This book has been a long time coming and having the right voice for it matters more than I can properly express.

This is not a technical manual. It is a book about communication — about how we talk to machines, and why doing it well matters for the planet and for people. I want listeners to feel that. I want the Year 7 sections to feel warm and clear. I want the University sections to feel precise and grounded.

Read your opening script. Look through the production rules. If anything feels off — anything at all — write to me before you record. There is no rush. Getting it right is everything.

— John Kenneally

Your opening script

This is the text you read at the start of the audiobook. Say it out loud — it should feel natural. If anything sounds awkward, mark it and let me know.

Opening — Read this before Chapter 1

Welcome to PromptCraft 1: Teaching Machines to Listen.

This book has one purpose: to show you how to communicate with artificial intelligence so clearly that it does exactly what you need — the first time.

At the centre is the C.R.A.F.T. method — five elements that turn a vague thought into a precise instruction: Context, Role, Action, Format, and Tone.

Every chapter appears in two versions: one written for students around twelve years old — plain, direct, with real examples. And one written for university students, professionals, and policy makers — with more depth, more evidence, and more formal language.

You can read both. You can choose one. The book meets you where you are.

I'm [narrator's name], and this is PromptCraft 1.

Production Rules

What you cut, keep, and adapt

Element Rule Notes
Activity boxes / exercises CUT Written-book only — skip in the audiobook
Tables ADAPT Read as a short prose list or summary
Year 7 + University sections KEEP Read both — they are the heart of the book
Phrases like "See Section X" CUT Not relevant in audio — omit cleanly
C.R.A.F.T. acronym letters (C, R, A, F, T) KEEP Spell them out when first introduced; use the word after
Industry acronyms (C.A.R.E.S., B.R.I.E.F., etc.) ADAPT Introduce spelling first, then use the word naturally
Data / statistics KEEP Read exactly as written — slow and clear
Callout boxes ADAPT Read the content; skip the box title unless it adds meaning
