A note before you start

I hope this guide provides the clearest way to communicate what I'm aiming for. I tried a few different approaches but found they only made things more complicated. As you've heard me say — keep the thing about the thing. This is my attempt to do exactly that.

I hope the notes and directions throughout the manuscript provide everything you need. Learning the audiobook production process has been both enjoyable and educational, and I've tried to make the manuscript as narration-friendly as possible.

I've removed tables that simply repeated or reinforced concepts already explained in the text. My rule has been: if a table only reiterates an existing idea, it doesn't need to be narrated. If you come across a table that has been missed, interrupts the flow, or hasn't been converted — please omit it, flag it for me, and continue with the next paragraph. We can address it later.

And Deborah — I would appreciate your opinion on the acronym question too. Do you think C.R.A.F.T. and the other acronyms should remain in English across all language editions, or should each edition develop native-language acronyms? Your instinct matters here.

— John Kenneally

How the Manuscript Is Being Sent

Read this before anything else

The book is divided into six parts. You will receive them one at a time. This is deliberate. The goal is not to hand you the entire manuscript and leave you to work through it alone. The goal is to keep the process moving, keep the energy high, and give John the opportunity to listen, adjust, and improve before the next part begins.

Six natural breaks. Six opportunities to check in. Six chances to catch anything that needs fixing before it becomes a habit.

📌 Part 1 is a walk-through. Not a final take.

John is not expecting studio-quality audio from Part 1. He is expecting a first recording that gets you comfortable with the manuscript and the process, reveals anything unexpected — pacing, terminology, flow issues, places where the text doesn't feel natural to speak — and gives John something to listen to before Part 2 begins.

Deborah — if Part 1 turns out to be first-take quality, that's a bonus. If it surfaces things to fix, that's exactly what it's for.

🎙️ English Opening — Deborah Arnell

[TITLE CARD — spoken] PromptCraft 1. Teaching Machines to Listen. Written by John Kenneally. [INTRO — spoken] PromptCraft is a sibling to prompt engineering — but far more personal. Where prompt engineering focuses on technical precision, PromptCraft focuses on you. Your words. Your intent. Your way of thinking. The system runs on a linguistic engine called C.R.A.F.T. — Context, Role, Action, Format, Tone — five elements that shape every interaction you have with AI. This book is not for people who simply want to use AI better. There are thousands of products out there — including this one — that achieve that result in thousands of different ways. What PromptCraft 1 gives you is something different. It gives you the ability to improve AI on your terms — in any situation, on any given day. More concisely. More efficiently. More personally. More controlled. For your business. For your home. For your family. Using your own words as the foundation of what comes back. This book explains what each element of C.R.A.F.T. does, why it works, and how to apply it across any field or discipline. Let's begin.
